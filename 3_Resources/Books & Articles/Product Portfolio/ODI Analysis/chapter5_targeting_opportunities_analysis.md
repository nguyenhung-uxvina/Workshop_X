## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Chapter 5 of "What Customers Want" presents **Targeting Opportunities for Growth** as the fifth step in the Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI) process. The core insight: once opportunities are identified through outcome scoring, companies must *surgically select* which underserved and overserved outcomes to pursue—targeting opportunities, not customers.

**Key Principle:** Add function/performance in underserved areas; reduce cost/function in overserved areas. Deliver all performance that can be absorbed—but no more.

## PART 1: CONCEPTUAL CLARIFICATION

### 1.1 What Is Different About Targeting for Innovation?

**Traditional Targeting:** Select customers or market segments to pursue.  
**ODI Targeting:** Select *opportunities* (underserved/overserved outcomes) to pursue.

| Traditional Approach | ODI Targeting Approach |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| "Which customers should we target?" | "Which outcomes should we improve?" |
| Demographic segmentation | Outcome-based segmentation |
| Feature-driven development | Outcome-driven development |
| Guessing at value | Measuring opportunity scores |

**Why This Matters:** Companies often fail by targeting opportunities they *can* address rather than those they *should* address. The ODI approach uses quantified opportunity scores to identify high-impact targets with surgical precision.

### 2.2 Feedback Loop Analysis

**Reinforcing Loop R1: "Success Breeds Success"**
```
[Target Right Opportunities] +→ [Customer Value Created] +→ [Market Share Gain]
         ↑                                                        │
         │                    +→ [Resources for Next Cycle] ←────+
         │                              │
         +───────────────────────────────+
```
*Polarity: All positive (+) = Reinforcing (R)*
*Behavior: Exponential growth when targeting right opportunities*

**Reinforcing Loop R2: "Wrong Target Spiral"**
```
[Target Wrong Opportunities] +→ [Resources Wasted] +→ [Less Available for Right Targets]
         ↑                                                              │
         │                    +→ [Pressure to Show Progress] ←──────────+
         │                              │
         +──────────────────────────────+
```
*Behavior: Vicious cycle when targeting overserved or low-opportunity outcomes*

**Balancing Loop B1: "Market Saturation"**
```
[Market Share Gain] +→ [Market Coverage] +→ [Remaining Opportunity Gap]
         ↑                                            │
         │                                     (−)    ↓
         +←──────── [Difficulty of Further Gains] ←───+
```
*Behavior: Growth slows as easy opportunities get addressed*

### 2.3 System Archetypes Detected

**Archetype 1: "Shifting the Burden"**
- **Pattern:** Companies target opportunities they CAN address (easier) rather than those they SHOULD address (harder but higher impact)
- **Quick Fix:** Focus on familiar technologies
- **Root Solution:** Develop new competencies for high-opportunity outcomes
- **ODI Remedy:** Opportunity scores reveal where resources SHOULD go

**Archetype 2: "Success to the Successful"**
- **Pattern:** Resources flow to projects showing progress, starving others
- **Risk:** High-opportunity projects may need more time/resources initially
- **ODI Remedy:** Prioritize by opportunity score, not short-term metrics

**Archetype 3: "Drifting Goals"**
- **Pattern:** When high-opportunity outcomes seem hard, companies lower aspirations
- **Risk:** "Good enough" becomes standard, underserved outcomes stay underserved
- **ODI Remedy:** Quantified opportunity scores hold organizations accountable

## PART 5: META-LEARNING FRAMEWORK

### 5.1 Feynman Technique: Explain Targeting to a Novice

**Simple Explanation:**

"Imagine you run a restaurant. You could ask customers 'What do you want?' and get answers like 'good food' (not helpful). Or you could watch what they struggle with and measure it.

ODI targeting is like discovering that 80% of customers struggle to find vegetarian options (underserved) and 90% think the bread basket is unnecessary (overserved). 

Instead of targeting 'health-conscious customers' (demographic), you target 'making vegetarian options easier to find' (outcome).

For defense training systems, instead of targeting 'special forces units' (customer), you target 'minimize time to achieve qualification' (outcome). Every product gets evaluated against this outcome."

### 5.2 Chunking the Targeting Framework

**Chunk 1: Opportunity Definition**
- Opportunity = Important outcome + Not well satisfied
- Score = Importance + Max(Importance - Satisfaction, 0)
- Score >12 = Pursue, Score <10 = Consider cost reduction

**Chunk 2: Broad-Market Opportunities**
- Theme opportunities: Related outcomes → unified positioning
- Unrelated opportunities: Multiple high-scores → feature combination
- Big opportunity: Score >15 → new ancillary product
- Overserved: Satisfaction > Importance → reduce cost
- Technology gaps: Future opportunities → R&D targeting

**Chunk 3: Segment-Specific Strategies**
- Spanning: Find opportunities common to 50%+ of segments
- Platform: Common features for common opportunities
- Sequencing: Least demanding segment first
- Price points: Map performance vs. price by segment

**Chunk 4: Competitive Position**
- Unique position = First to address underserved outcomes
- Valued position = Outcomes that customers prioritize
- Sustainable position = Theme that competitors can't easily copy

### 7.1 Root Causes of Targeting Failure

| Failure Mode | Description | System Archetype | Countermeasure |
|-------------|-------------|------------------|----------------|
| **Confirmation Bias** | Commission research to validate existing beliefs | Seeking Wrong Goal | Define success as "findings acted upon" not "hypothesis confirmed" |
| **Career Protection** | Refute results that threaten managers | Shifting Burden | Create psychological safety for uncomfortable truths |
| **Competency Avoidance** | Reject opportunities requiring new skills | Drifting Goals | Frame skill development as strategic investment |
| **Methodology Attack** | Discredit research to avoid implications | Fixes That Fail | Pre-commit to act on results before study begins |

### 7.2 Systemic Solutions

**L3 - Goal Redefinition:**
"Our goal is to address the highest-opportunity outcomes, not to confirm what we already believe."

**L5 - Policy Changes:**
- Mandatory pre-commitment: "We will pursue top 3 opportunities regardless of findings"
- Budget allocation tied to opportunity scores
- Performance reviews include "opportunities addressed" metrics

**L6 - Information Flow:**
- Make opportunity scores visible to all stakeholders
- Create accountability for ignoring high-score opportunities
- Regular reviews of "opportunities we didn't pursue and why"
