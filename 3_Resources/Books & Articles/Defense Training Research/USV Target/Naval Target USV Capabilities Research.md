

# **Unmanned Surface Vehicles in Modern Naval Warfare: A Comprehensive Analysis of Threat Simulation and Training Platforms**

## **I. Strategic Context and Target Platform Analysis**

The effectiveness of modern naval forces hinges on their ability to neutralize threats that exploit speed, maneuverability, and numerical advantage in littoral and open-ocean environments. Unmanned Surface Vehicles (USVs) designed as high-fidelity targets are now indispensable for maintaining combat readiness, serving as dynamic surrogates for real-world adversaries, particularly Fast Attack Craft (FAC) and Fast Inshore Attack Craft (FIAC).

### **A. The Evolving Threat Landscape: Mandating Realistic FIAC/FAC Simulation**

The proliferation of advanced maritime adversaries, often utilizing asymmetric tactics involving small, high-speed craft, necessitates that naval forces validate both kinetic and non-kinetic defensive systems against targets that accurately mirror adversarial behavior.1 High-fidelity threat replication requires that target USVs execute the full spectrum of high-speed naval tactics, including complex crossing patterns, advanced zig-zag evasive maneuvers, and aggressive straight-on high-speed attacks.1 The primary goal of this rigorous doctrinal focus is to push shipboard tracking and engagement systems to their operational limits, ensuring systems can acquire and neutralize highly dynamic surface threats.

### **B. Technical Benchmarking of Leading Target Systems: Hammerhead vs. C-Target**

Two primary families of target USVs dominate the market, each exhibiting distinct design philosophies optimized for different training demands.

#### **QinetiQ Hammerhead USV-T**

The Hammerhead USV-T is explicitly engineered to simulate FIACs and excel in replicating FAC naval threats.1 Its operational performance is market-leading, achieving speeds of up to 35 knots in Sea State (SS) 3 and speeds greater than 35 knots in SS2 and below, powered by a 135 hp gas 3.0L Merc engine.1 This robust speed in moderate sea states provides a significant performance increase over legacy target systems. Furthermore, the Hammerhead offers substantial endurance, providing 8 hours of operation at 20 knots and 5 hours at 30 knots, allowing for extended live-fire exercises with a mission range exceeding 100 kilometers (54 nautical miles).2 Its hull length is 5.2m (17ft) without the drive, and its modularity is supported by internal payload locations and optional single or double-tiered payload mounting shelves.1

#### **L3Harris/ASV C-Target Family**

The C-Target family (including models C-Target 3, 6, 9, and 13\) emphasizes logistical efficiency alongside high maneuverability.4 These systems utilize highly survivable and repairable rugged aluminum hulls.4 The C-Target 3 is particularly noteworthy for its innovative two-part hull design, which allows four complete systems to fit within a standard 20-foot container, prioritizing rapid global deployment and simplified logistics.4 The C-Target 13 ASV is a high-speed vehicle configurable for either direct fire targeting or as a tow platform for other targets, and is uniquely configurable between manned and unmanned operational modes.6

The disparity in design—the Hammerhead focusing on maximum sustained performance for high-intensity exercises, versus the C-Target prioritizing logistical reach and containerization—reflects a strategic trade-off. The Hammerhead is optimized for full-scale, blue-water exercises requiring maximum, sustained threat realism, whereas the C-Target favors a smaller logistical footprint, making it ideal for rapid deployment and unit-level training where ease of transport and quick repair are paramount.

The future role of target USVs extends beyond simple threat replication. These systems are proving their viability as "Threat Surrogates" capable of integrated offensive and defensive maneuvers. For instance, the Common Unmanned Surface Vehicle (CUSV) was successfully employed by the U.S. Navy and the Republic of Korea (ROK) Navy to launch South Korea's Poniard rockets during RIMPAC 2024\.7 This capability validates the concept of integrating kinetic or electronic attack capabilities onto USV platforms, supporting the transition from a linear "Kill Chain" to a distributed "Kill Web" doctrine where autonomous platforms contribute dynamically to the engagement and not merely as passive targets.9

## **II. Advanced Gunnery and Weapon System Effectiveness Training**

Target USVs are essential for validating the operational readiness of high-value naval weapon systems against realistic maneuvering threats.

### **A. Maneuver Profiles and Target Challenge Generation**

The Hammerhead USV-T is specifically engineered to replicate the dynamic, unpredictable movement of hostile small craft, employing high-speed attacks, complex crossing patterns, and advanced zig-zag evasive maneuvers necessary to challenge radar tracking and kinetic solutions.1 By maintaining high speeds (35+ knots) even in moderate sea states (SS3), these targets force weapons systems, including Close-In Weapon Systems (CIWS) and high-speed Surface-to-Surface Missiles (SSMs), to execute challenging intercepts against highly dynamic targets, thereby testing the limits of shipboard fire control.1

### **B. Weapon System Certification and Readiness Testing**

Target USVs facilitate the testing of virtually all major shipboard weapon categories, ensuring operational readiness across the kinetic spectrum.1 This comprehensive test matrix includes:

* **Surface-to-Surface Missiles (SSM):** Harpoon, Exocet, RBS 15\.  
* **Surface-to-Air Missiles (in surface-to-surface mode):** NATO Sea Sparrow, Evolved Sea Sparrow (ESSM), Standard SM 1, Standard SM 2 Block III A.  
* **Naval Guns/CIWS:** Phalanx, 25-30 Bushmaster, 40mm Bofors, 57mm Bofors Mk III, 76mm Oto Melara SRGM, and 127mm guns.1

While the target USVs themselves are low-cost platforms intended to mimic inexpensive threats, their true value lies in validating the successful processing, tracking, prioritization, and allocation of multi-million-dollar interceptors (such as Harpoon or ESSM) against a difficult-to-track, small, high-speed target in a complex littoral environment. The expense of the USV target is justified by the mission-critical validation of the ship’s complex Combat System.

For gunnery training, the survivability and cost-effectiveness of the target are paramount. The design focus on rugged aluminum hulls and high repairability 4 confirms that USVs are engineered to withstand multiple low-caliber engagements, maximizing the training value per dollar spent and reserving destructive testing for high-value kinetic missile validation events.

### **C. Live-Fire Case Studies: USV as a Launch Platform**

The utility of target USV chassis was recently demonstrated during RIMPAC 2024\. The Textron Systems Common Unmanned Surface Vehicle (CUSV) was successfully utilized by the U.S. Navy and the ROK Navy to launch and test South Korea's LIG Nex1 Poniard rockets.7 The CUSV, equipped with six Poniard rockets, successfully engaged a separate target during the exercise.8 This event validated the platform’s capacity for rapid integration of low-cost guided munitions and its robustness for dual-role activities, confirming its viability beyond passive target status.

## **III. Swarm Dynamics and Multi-Threat Environment Simulation**

Simulating high-density swarm attacks is crucial for validating a ship's ability to manage overwhelming kinetic and sensor saturation.

### **A. Architecture of USV Swarm Control and Capacity**

The capability of target USVs to replicate saturation attacks varies significantly between platforms. The Hammerhead USV-T is explicitly designed to simulate a FIAC in a multi-vehicle swarm of up to **40 vehicles**.1 This large-scale coordination is managed by the Universal Target Control System (UTCS), which is designed to communicate concurrently with up to sixteen craft.10 This capacity is optimized for large-scale force-on-force testing where the strategic objective is to overwhelm naval defenses through numerical saturation.

In contrast, the L3Harris C-Target family, managed by the ASView-T Control System, enables coordinated swarm operation but is designed to command up to **four target vessels** over a single radio network.6 This divergence in control architecture suggests the C-Target system prioritizes highly controlled, complex maneuvering involving a small number of threats (simulating high-priority targets or leader-follower dynamics), rather than sheer numerical saturation required for testing major warship defenses against true "grey zone" swarms.

### **B. Developing Anti-Swarm Tactics and Training Requirements**

Coordinating dozens of targets requires robust autonomous behaviors to execute synchronized, multi-axis attacks that necessitate a layered defensive response.1 Swarm training forces crews to develop complex anti-swarm tactics, specifically challenging the high-speed detection, identification, and tracking of multiple objects in high-clutter environments. Ongoing research and development efforts, such as those conducted by the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory, are focused on advancing collaborative, autonomous USV behaviors to higher speeds and significantly larger numbers of vessels, continuously pushing the boundaries of operational swarm realism.11

### **C. Sensor Signature Customization and Threat Realism**

Target USVs utilize customizable profiles to ensure they present a convincing likeness across sensor domains, thereby ensuring maximum training value.

#### **Radar Cross Section (RCS) Augmentation**

To accurately test shipboard radar and fire control systems, USVs are equipped with significant signature enhancements.1 The Hammerhead, for example, offers Passive Radar Augmentation ranging from 20 to 500 square meters in the I-Band, allowing it to realistically mimic targets ranging from small fishing vessels to larger Fast Attack Craft at different ranges.1 Furthermore, Active Radar Augmentation via an RF-SAS system (Radio Frequency Signature Augmentation System) is available, which is critical for testing against complex jamming or deceptive electronic warfare scenarios.1

#### **Thermal/Infrared (IR) and Visual Signatures**

Visual enhancements include remote control over standard equipment such as smoke, flags, strobe lights, and flares.1 While the Hammerhead can be equipped with "forward looking IR signatures" 10, and IR target acquisition is paramount for modern IR search and track (IRST) systems 12, explicit details regarding dedicated **IR generation** or quantified thermal augmentation systems are not consistently detailed in technical specifications.1

This potential gap in thermal realism is a critical vulnerability in passive sensor training. IRST systems rely heavily on the engine heat and exhaust signature—the primary discriminators for small targets.12 If thermal realism is underdeveloped, relying solely on unaugmented engine signatures, training may fail to accurately validate EO/IR tracking systems and passive targeting solutions. Achieving full threat simulation realism necessitates the integration of dedicated thermal modeling or high-fidelity IR emitter systems.

## **IV. Command, Control, and Decision-Making Assessment (C2)**

The integration of USV targets into large-scale exercises allows for a robust assessment of shipboard command, control, and decision-making processes under realistic multi-threat conditions.

### **A. USV Data Integration into the Naval Kill Web**

USVs operate as crucial, long-dwell sensor nodes, classifying targets via multiple modalities, such as acoustic and AIS data, providing real-time "tipping and cueing" to other assets (e.g., UAVs).9 The effective coordination of these heterogeneous systems demands robust C2 architectures equipped with AI-driven tools. The core challenge in utilizing autonomous USVs is not simple system interoperability but rather achieving seamless **real-time data merging** to maintain accurate situational awareness and enable distributed decision-making.9 This requirement necessitates a shift in C2 training emphasis, moving beyond traditional voice and data link procedures toward validating machine-to-machine data handling and the performance of AI-assisted decision support systems. Autonomous USVs must be fully integrated into existing C2 structures, necessitating system modifications and the adoption of new autonomy guidelines based on mission proximity and environment.14

### **B. C2 System Interoperability and Modeling (SoS Engineering)**

The development and integration of these complex sensor, weapon, and C2 systems are guided by System of Systems (SoS) engineering principles to specifically address historical deficiencies in strike-force interoperability.15 Modeling and Simulation (M\&S) is a core tool used to support requirements definition, analyze complex Force Package configurations, and validate integration across an effects chain. M\&S supports the assessment of functional and performance characteristics, ensuring new autonomous systems are compatible with existing naval architecture.15

### **C. Methodology for Decision-Making Assessment**

Exercises using high-density USV swarms are essential for testing the cognitive resilience of naval decision-makers under extreme pressure.16 These complex scenarios are designed to expose command teams to common cognitive biases. For example, the overwhelming sensor inputs from a saturation attack can induce **target fixation**—a mental state where intense focus on one priority threat causes crucial secondary information to be filtered out—or **confirmation bias**, leading decision-makers to interpret ambiguous sensor returns according to what they expect to see.16

C2 assessment must move beyond simple kinetic outcomes (kill counts) to evaluate the efficiency and accuracy of the command process itself. This requires rigorous post-exercise analysis and quantitative behavioral observation to identify and mitigate cognitive biases in real-time decision-making. The assessment criteria must measure how effectively watchstanders utilize AI cueing and fuse data to order responses, linking human performance directly to the M\&S frameworks that define the operational envelope.9

Recent multinational exercises, such as Dynamic Messenger and UNITAS 2025, have validated this approach, deploying Global Autonomous Reconnaissance Craft (GARC) USVs to train against enemy drone swarms.17 These events focused on coordinated strikes and rapid threat response, driven by the critical need to test autonomous fleets in defense of high-value infrastructure.17

## **V. Non-Lethal Training Applications and Future Operational Concepts**

Target USVs are also essential for simulating suspicious or non-compliant vessels for non-lethal training, specifically in Visit, Board, Search, and Seizure (VBSS) and Maritime Interdiction Operations (MIO) training.

### **A. Maritime Interdiction Operations (MIO) / VBSS Training Realism**

While high-fidelity MIO training often uses large, actual cargo ships to practice physical boarding, searching, and seizing 18, target USVs fill the critical gap of simulating the high-speed, non-compliant target necessary for the initial interception phase. This training focuses on the fundamentals of boarding team safety, structured risk assessment, tactical procedures, and the safe application of the Use of Force (UOF) continuum in complex maritime law enforcement scenarios.20

### **B. GPS Tracking and Vulnerability Drills**

The sophisticated dynamic models used for target point tracking 21 allow USVs to execute complex and unpredictable high-speed evasion patterns, challenging interception teams. The USV's ability to coordinate in various formations (e.g., circular arrays, leader-follower dynamics) 22 enables training teams to refine complex interception geometry, including maintaining line-of-sight and respecting Field of View (FOV) constraints while closing on a moving target.22 The integration of GPS tracking allows operators to run vulnerability model scenarios, simulating the real-time degradation of the target vessel’s speed or handling based on "simulated damage" applied during the interception and tracking phase.

Target USVs enhance MIO training by shifting the focus to the **high-speed interception and containment dynamics** of a small, fast, non-compliant target. By accurately replicating realistic evasion maneuvers using dynamic control systems, USVs allow both cutter crews and boarding teams to refine pre-interdiction procedures, including establishing effective blocking formations and safely disabling or coercing the target before the physical boarding commences.21

To maximize training realism, target USVs should integrate non-lethal payload options, such as high-resolution video telemetry, allowing the command structure to gather real-time intelligence for accurate identification calls prior to engagement. Furthermore, platforms like the C-Target 13, which are configurable between manned and unmanned modes 6, are invaluable for practicing human-in-the-loop scenarios that simulate non-compliant crews or active resistance, adding crucial realism to MIO exercises.

## **VI. Conclusion**

Target Unmanned Surface Vehicles are integral to validating modern naval readiness across the operational spectrum, providing a dynamic, measurable, and repeatable means of threat simulation. The technological benchmark demonstrates platforms capable of replicating 40-vehicle swarm attacks (Hammerhead) and platforms optimized for logistical deployment (C-Target), catering to both high-end kinetic validation and global unit training needs.

The primary challenges moving forward reside in C2 modernization and achieving complete sensor realism. Integrating USVs into the naval Kill Web demands that C2 architectures prioritize real-time data fusion and AI-assisted decision support, moving past basic interoperability toward true command resilience under cognitive stress. Simultaneously, the deficiency in dedicated IR/thermal signature generation must be addressed to ensure that passive sensing systems are adequately challenged, thereby guaranteeing the effectiveness of every layer of the modern naval defense system.
