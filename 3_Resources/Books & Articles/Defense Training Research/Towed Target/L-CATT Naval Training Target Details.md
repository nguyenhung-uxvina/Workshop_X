

# **The QinetiQ L-CATT™: A Strategic Analysis of the Low-Cost Catamaran Tow Target for Advanced Naval Threat Emulation**

## **I. Executive Summary: The Strategic Value Proposition of the L-CATT™**

### **1.1. Core Findings**

The QinetiQ L-CATT™ (Low-Cost Catamaran Tow Target) is a specialized, 4.8-meter, purpose-built asset engineered for naval training and definitive weapon systems Test and Evaluation (T\&E).1 Designed with two rota-molded polythene hulls, the L-CATT is categorized fundamentally as an **expendable "kill" target**.2 Its primary function is to serve as the terminal engagement point during live-fire exercises, validating the performance of sophisticated kinetic weapon systems against realistic maritime threat profiles.4

### **1.2. Capability Multiplier**

The strategic significance of the L-CATT is defined by its seamless integration with and deployment by QinetiQ’s portfolio of high-performance Uncrewed Surface Vehicles (USVs), specifically the Sprite II, Hammerhead, and Barracuda platforms.1 This configuration is critical because the USVs, such as the Hammerhead, provide the necessary high-speed (up to 40 knots in Sea State 2\) and complex maneuverability (including zig-zag and evasive patterns) required to simulate modern threats like Fast Attack Craft (FAC) and Fast Inshore Attack Craft (FIAC).6 The L-CATT functions as the passive, yet configurable, target towed by these sophisticated assets.

### **1.3. Recommendation**

The L-CATT represents a powerful cost-optimization mechanism within naval T\&E budgets. By utilizing an expendable, low-cost target for terminal destruction, military organizations can conduct high-fidelity kinetic weapon systems testing without incurring the prohibitive cost and asset risk associated with intentionally destroying or severely damaging the advanced, high-value USV tow platform.2 This methodology ensures that rigorous live-fire trials are fiscally responsible and maximize the return on investment for complex operational training and weapon assurance programs.

## **II. Technical Deep Dive: Design and Material Science for Expendable T\&E**

### **2.1. Physical Specifications and Hydrodynamic Profile**

The L-CATT measures 4.8 meters in overall length.1 Structurally, it employs a catamaran, or twin-hull, design. This configuration provides several inherent hydrodynamic advantages essential for a towed target, including enhanced stability and a reduced drag coefficient compared to traditional monohull or purely inflatable targets. These characteristics are essential for maintaining stable orientation and precise tracking geometry when the target is towed at the high speeds required to simulate contemporary Fast Attack Craft threats.6

### **2.2. Construction and Material Science: The Rota-Molded Polythene Advantage**

The L-CATT hulls are manufactured using rota-molded (rotational molding) polythene (polyethylene).1 The selection of this material and manufacturing process is foundational to the L-CATT’s operational philosophy. Rotational molding produces stress-free, seamless parts with uniform wall thickness, contributing to the asset’s robustness.

For an asset explicitly designed to be expendable, the material choice must strike a delicate balance between low acquisition cost, mass production capability, and sufficient durability to withstand the operational environment until the point of engagement. Polythene is exceptionally resilient, offering high resistance to impact and corrosive effects of long-term saltwater exposure.1 The material's durability ensures the L-CATT maintains structural integrity while being towed at high dynamic loads (potentially over 35 knots) and through various sea states, only becoming "expendable" when intentionally destroyed by weapon fire.6 This intentional trade-off—durable manufacturing of a low-cost, disposable asset—is what facilitates the high tempo, high-risk training missions that cannot be afforded by risking high-value USVs.

The design attributes of the L-CATT directly support its specialized role as a complementary component within a sophisticated naval target system.

## **III. Operational Doctrine: Integration with QinetiQ Autonomous Systems**

### **3.1. Compatibility and Broad Platform Utility**

A primary operational advantage of the L-CATT is its extensive compatibility. The target system is designed to interface with *all* QinetiQ uncrewed surface vehicles, ensuring seamless integration across the entire QinetiQ surface target portfolio.1 Furthermore, the L-CATT is compatible with other existing naval vessels, providing allied navies with flexibility for deployment regardless of their current fleet composition or existing support infrastructure.1 This broad compatibility maximizes the utility of the L-CATT across diverse operational environments and allied maritime forces worldwide.9

### **3.2. QinetiQ USV Tow Platforms: Speed, Maneuverability, and Threat Simulation**

The L-CATT achieves high-fidelity threat replication only when paired with its high-performance tow platforms. It is routinely towed behind the Sprite II, Hammerhead, and Barracuda USVs.1

The **Hammerhead USV** provides the kinetic energy and dynamic maneuvering required to simulate credible, rapidly evolving threats.6 Powered by a 3.0L MerCruiser engine, the Hammerhead delivers speeds of up to 40 knots in Sea State 2 and 35 knots in Sea State 3\.7 Crucially, the Hammerhead can execute complex, high-maneuverability patterns, including straight-on high-speed attacks, crossing patterns, and zig-zag evasion maneuvers, which are essential for replicating the behavior of a Fast Inshore Attack Craft (FIAC) or Fast Attack Craft (FAC).6 The towed L-CATT passively mirrors these high-speed dynamic profiles.

The **Barracuda USV** is another large, high-performance platform capable of speeds exceeding 36 knots.2 Its published key features explicitly state the ability to tow "cost-effective 'Kill' targets" 2, reinforcing the specific role of the L-CATT as the terminal target component within the T\&E system architecture.

The **Sprite II USV** provides flexibility and versatility, with different models capable of over 30 knots.10 For missions requiring greater separation between the high-value USV and the point of impact, larger Sprite II models can be fitted with an electric winch containing 500 meters of tow line.10 This feature is vital for range safety and scenario realism.

### **3.3. Mitigating Risk and Extending Engagement Range**

The combined USV/L-CATT architecture addresses a fundamental challenge in live-fire testing: mitigating asset risk while maintaining realism. The USV (e.g., Hammerhead or Barracuda) performs the complex, high-speed maneuver profile necessary to simulate the threat's approach and evasion (Phase 1: Approach). By deploying the L-CATT on a long tow line (up to 500 meters separation in some cases) 10, the expensive, sophisticated USV is safely positioned outside the hazard envelope, including the fragmentation and blast radius generated by kinetic warhead impact.

This doctrine ensures that the high-G maneuvering profiles needed for target acquisition and tracking validation are achieved, but the potentially destructive "kill shot" is absorbed by the low-cost, expendable L-CATT.2 This represents an evolved strategic approach to naval training: utilizing high-fidelity autonomous systems to safely *deliver* a realistic threat presentation, rather than risking them as the terminal target itself.

## **IV. Advanced Threat Replication and Payload Augmentation**

### **4.1. Role in Weapon Systems Testing and Evaluation (T\&E)**

The L-CATT is a dedicated asset for rigorous "weapon systems testing and evaluation" and military operational training.1 The ability to subject weapon systems to realistic engagement conditions is fundamental to weapon assurance, which involves proving operational safety, effective platform integration, and performance validation against modern threat parameters.4

The USV systems towing the L-CATT are used to test a comprehensive range of weapon types, demonstrating the target's utility across naval warfare domains. These weapons include Surface-to-Surface Missiles (such as Harpoon, Exocet, and RBS 15), Surface-to-Air Missiles used in surface-to-surface mode (like NATO Sea Sparrow), Air-to-Surface Missiles (such as AGM 65 Maverick), and heavy Naval Guns/Cannon and Close In Weapon Systems (CIWS) like Phalanx, 57mm Bofors Mk III, and 127mm guns.11 The successful deployment of the L-CATT in these high-stakes tests confirms its viability as a reliable, certifiable training and evaluation instrument.

### **4.2. Payloads and Signature Management for High-Fidelity Training**

The L-CATT is designed with optional payloads, allowing it to be equipped to "effectively replicate various naval threats".1 This payload customization is not merely an auxiliary feature; it is critical for high-fidelity engagement simulations in the context of modern guidance technology.

Contemporary naval weapon systems, particularly precision guided munitions and missiles, rely on advanced multi-mode seekers and robust guidance systems, sometimes employing Global Positioning System (GPS)/inertial navigation (like advanced JDAM variants that can engage moving maritime targets).12 A simple visual or hull-based target is insufficient to validate the performance of a sophisticated weapon system's entire kill chain, which spans detection, tracking, lock-on, and guidance.

Therefore, the optional payloads serve to bridge the signature gap between the simple polythene hull and a credible military vessel by generating realistic threat signatures. These augmentations likely include enhancements for Radar Cross Section (RCS) management, Infrared (IR) heat generation, and potentially acoustic or electronic emissions management.8 QinetiQ’s broader expertise in target systems engineering, including the integration of advanced payloads for threat replication in airborne systems 14, confirms the organizational capability to adapt the L-CATT to match evolving adversary capabilities and meet stringent T\&E requirements for sensors and guidance systems.

## **V. Comparative Market Analysis and Strategic Positioning**

### **5.1. Positioning within the QinetiQ Portfolio**

QinetiQ Target Systems is recognized as a world-leading provider of uncrewed air and surface targets designed specifically for Threat Representation.3 Within this specialized portfolio, the L-CATT is strategically positioned as a low-cost, expendable, and complementary tow target.5

The L-CATT operates alongside larger, more complex USV systems (Hammerhead, Barracuda) and other towed targets like the High Speed Inflatable Towed Targets (HSITT).5 Its most crucial strategic function is enabling affordable, scalable force-on-force training. The Hammerhead USV, for example, is capable of controlling multi-vehicle swarm attacks of up to 40 targets simultaneously.5 When conducting live-fire training against such a swarm, a solution that provides low-cost, terminal kinetic engagement points is paramount. The L-CATT, as the designated "cost-effective 'Kill' target," provides the required large-scale, low-cost terminal component attached to the fewer, high-value USV controllers. This strategy is essential for realizing affordable, high-volume T\&E events necessary for validating countermeasures against contemporary swarm tactics.

### **5.2. Competitive Landscape: Comparison with Traditional Inflatables**

Traditional alternatives to the L-CATT exist, primarily in the form of Naval Gunnery Target Balloons (NavTGTs), such as the GeoDataSys "Killer Banana" target.16 These are typically large (e.g., 6.7 meters), air-inflated targets made of PVC vinyl film, intended for basic gunnery skills and target acquisition.16

The performance differential between the L-CATT and these traditional inflatables is substantial and operationally significant. Traditional balloons are limited to speeds of approximately 20 knots maximum, and only in calmer water or moderate sea states.17 Modern Fast Inshore Attack Craft (FIAC) threats are inherently high-speed. Limiting training engagements to a 20-knot target fails to accurately validate a weapon system's performance, fire control system stability, and crew reaction time against a realistic, rapidly closing threat profile.7

By contrast, the rigid, hydrodynamic polythene L-CATT, when towed by platforms like the Hammerhead, operates effectively in the 35-40 knot range, meeting the requisite speed and dynamic performance for advanced threat emulation.7 Furthermore, while inflatables rely on passive radar reflection (e.g., aluminum foil corner reflectors) 16, the L-CATT supports configurable, active payloads, placing it firmly in the category of advanced kinetic target systems necessary for modern missile and CIWS testing, rather than simple gunnery practice.

### **5.3. Competitive Landscape: Comparison with Competitor USVs**

A comparison must also be drawn with highly survivable competitor Unmanned Surface Vehicles, such as the L3Harris C-Target 13 ASV.18 The C-Target 13 is a large, high-speed USV target boat, constructed with aluminum hulls for high survivability and ease of repair.18 It can be configured to act as a direct fire target or a tow boat.18

This contrast highlights differing strategic philosophies regarding terminal target risk:

1. **L3Harris C-Target Philosophy:** Focuses on the survivable, reusable target (aluminum hull) that absorbs non-lethal impacts and provides command and control assessment capabilities.18 While highly effective for certain training scenarios, intentionally destroying this high-value, durable asset for definitive "kill" validation is fiscally inefficient.  
2. **QinetiQ L-CATT/USV Philosophy:** Focuses on separating the sophisticated maneuvering platform from the disposable terminal target. The high-value USV (Hammerhead/Barracuda) executes the complex path, while the low-cost, expendable L-CATT absorbs the kinetic impact.

The QinetiQ model offers a potentially superior cost-efficiency for missions requiring the definitive destruction of a target to certify weapon effectiveness (Weapon Assurance). The L-CATT enables continuous, high-volume live-fire T\&E without the recurrent capital expense of replacing or heavily repairing expensive, primary USV platforms.

## **VI. Strategic and Fiscal Recommendations for Naval Programs**

### **6.1. Cost-Benefit Analysis of Expendable Assets**

The defining characteristic of the L-CATT is its "low-cost and expendable" nature.1 In an era where naval budget pressures are substantial, with projected US Navy shipbuilding costs estimated to average $40 billion annually over the next 30 years 19, minimizing operational risk and maximizing training efficiency is paramount.

By making the L-CATT disposable, the operational maintenance (O\&M) and capital costs associated with reusable, sophisticated autonomous targets for destructive tests are largely eliminated. This strategy effectively ring-fences high-value USV platforms from kinetic engagement risk, thereby decreasing their total cost of ownership and increasing their overall availability for complex maneuvering exercises.3 The expendability of the L-CATT translates directly into budget optimization, ensuring that the substantial investment in advanced missiles and munitions testing is supported by a robust, yet cost-effective, terminal target solution.

### **6.2. Enhancing Operational Readiness through Realistic T\&E**

Operational readiness hinges on the provision of threat emulation services that provide realistic representation of modern threats.3 The L-CATT, coupled with the high-speed and maneuvering capabilities of its tow platforms (up to 40 knots) 7, ensures that training environments accurately reflect the kinematic profiles of contemporary threats like swarming FIACs.

The L-CATT provides the necessary mechanism for definitive engagement verification, allowing naval forces to acquire reliable evidence that their weapons and platforms will perform as required against the target.4 This rigor in T\&E is essential to deliver effective fighting capability, ensuring personnel are fully trained and equipment is fully tested prior to deployment.3

### **6.3. Recommendations for Procurement and Deployment Strategy**

For naval programs, procurement strategy should recognize the L-CATT not merely as a peripheral consumable, but as a crucial enabler for high-value mission assurance activities. Procurement planning should integrate L-CATT stock levels directly with projected high-cost munition testing cycles, particularly those involving missile and CIWS assurance.4

Furthermore, standardized operational procedures should leverage the L-CATT’s ability to be towed at long ranges. Utilizing the 500-meter tow line capability, available on larger Sprite II platforms 10, allows training commands to maximize the safety separation between the crewed/uncrewed tow platform and the kinetic impact point. This facilitates training in layered defense scenarios and ensures the USV can remain safely positioned for post-impact data collection, supporting the overall T\&E data validation process.

### **6.4. Outlook: The Future Role in Autonomous Systems (RAS) Doctrine**

QinetiQ is heavily invested in Robotics and Autonomous Systems (RAS) development across land, sea, and air.8 The L-CATT's continued relevance is secured by its modular design and compatibility with evolving USV platforms.

The target’s capacity for rapid reconfiguration and its adaptable payload interface future-proof the system against emerging adversary threat profiles. As new detection, tracking, or electronic warfare capabilities emerge, the L-CATT can be rapidly equipped with updated signature augmentation payloads to simulate these threats.14 This modularity ensures that the L-CATT remains a crucial component in validating future combat systems and maintains the necessary competitive edge in naval threat emulation services.

## **VII. Conclusion: The L-CATT as an Indispensable Component of Modern Naval T\&E**

The QinetiQ L-CATT™ is strategically indispensable to modern naval T\&E, transcending the role of a mere target to become a critical force multiplier in training doctrine. Its robust, 4.8-meter, rota-molded catamaran structure allows it to withstand high-speed towing by sophisticated USVs like the Hammerhead and Barracuda, enabling the creation of dynamic, 35-40 knot threat profiles.7

The core value proposition lies in its expendable nature and low cost, which protects the significant capital investment in advanced USV maneuvering platforms while enabling high-volume, kinetic engagement testing necessary for weapon assurance against missiles and naval guns.2 By supporting configurable payloads, the L-CATT provides the high-fidelity signature management required to validate modern multi-mode guidance systems, a capability that distinguishes it sharply from traditional low-speed inflatable targets. The L-CATT ensures that high-risk naval training remains relevant, definitive, and fiscally responsible.
